    OpenDNA Tutorials¶

    Step-by-step walkthroughs for common protein engineering tasks.

    Tutorial 1: Analyze Your First Protein (5 minutes)¶

    Goal: Learn the basic OpenDNA workflow on a real protein.

    Steps¶

    1. Start the platform (if not already running):
    2. Terminal 1: python -c "from opendna.api.server import start_server; start_server(port=8765)"
    3. Terminal 2: cd ui && npm run dev

    4. Browser: http://localhost:5173

    5. Import ubiquitin from the famous proteins list:

    6. Click the Import tab in the sidebar
    7. Click ubiquitin in the famous proteins grid
    8. You should see a green toast: "UBC_HUMAN — Loaded 685 aa from Homo sapiens"

    9. The sidebar switches to the Tools tab automatically

    10. Score the sequence instantly (no model download needed):

    11. Click Score Protein

    12. A score card appears showing the overall quality and breakdown

    13. Run the full analysis suite:

    14. Click Full Analysis Suite
    15. Browse through ~12 sections of analysis

    16. Look for: disorder prediction (UBC has some disorder), aggregation risk, phosphorylation sites

    17. Try a famous mutation:

    18. In the Mutate Active Protein field, type K48R
    19. Click Apply Mutation & Refold
    20. This is the famous K48R mutation that disrupts polyubiquitin chain formation

    What you learned¶

    • How to import proteins from UniProt
    • The instant scoring system
    • The comprehensive analysis suite
    • How to apply point mutations

    Tutorial 2: Predict and Visualize a 3D Structure (10 minutes)¶

    Goal: Fold a small protein and explore the 3D viewer.

    Prerequisites¶

    First-time only: ESMFold model will download (~8 GB). Make sure you have a fast internet connection and ~10 GB free disk space.

    Steps¶

    1. Use a small test sequence:
    2. Paste this in the Tools tab: 
      MKTVRQERLKSIVRILERSKEPVSGAQLAEELS

    3. That's 33 residues, perfect for a quick fold

    4. Click Predict Structure

    5. First time: ESMFold downloads (~8 GB, watch the API terminal)
    6. Subsequent runs: instant model load
    7. For 33 residues on CPU: ~1-2 minutes

    8. For 33 residues on GPU: ~5 seconds

    9. Explore the 3D structure:

    10. Drag the protein to rotate
    11. Scroll to zoom

    12. Right-click drag to pan

    13. Switch to confidence coloring:

    14. Click pLDDT colors at the bottom-left
    15. Blue regions are high confidence

    16. Orange/red regions are low confidence (often loops or disordered)

    17. Save the structure:

    18. Click Save PDB in the header
    19. The PDB file downloads to your computer
    20. You can open it in any other molecular viewer (PyMOL, ChimeraX, etc.)

    What you learned¶

    • How to fold proteins with ESMFold
    • 3D viewer controls
    • Confidence visualization
    • Exporting PDB files

    Tutorial 3: Design Alternative Sequences with ESM-IF1 (15 minutes)¶

    Goal: Take a folded protein and generate alternative sequences that should fold into the same shape.

    Steps¶

    1. First, fold a protein (use Tutorial 2's sequence or any other)

    2. Click "Design 10 Sequences (ESM-IF1)"

    3. First time: ESM-IF1 model downloads (~600 MB)
    4. Generates 10 alternative sequences

    5. Each is scored by recovery (% of residues matching the original)

    6. Review the results:

    7. A panel appears with the 10 candidates ranked by score
    8. Notice the recovery percentages (typically 4-30% with default temperature)
    9. High recovery = conservative redesign

    10. Low recovery = aggressive redesign of the same fold

    11. Verify a candidate by folding it:

    12. Click Fold on candidate #1
    13. This folds the new sequence

    14. Add it to your structures list

    15. Compare original vs designed:

    16. Switch to the Structures tab
    17. Click vs on the original structure
    18. The viewer splits to show both side-by-side
    19. Visually compare — they should have similar topology if ESM-IF1 worked

    What you learned¶

    • How inverse folding works
    • Sequence recovery interpretation
    • How to verify designed sequences by re-folding
    • Side-by-side structure comparison

    Tutorial 4: Iterative Design Loop (30 minutes)¶

    Goal: Use OpenDNA's killer feature — automated protein optimization over multiple rounds.

    Steps¶

    1. Pick a starting sequence: 

      MKTVRQERLKSIVRILER
      (18 residues — small enough to iterate quickly on CPU)

    2. Configure iterative design:

    3. In the Iterative Design Loop section
    4. Set rounds: 3 and candidates: 5

    5. That's 15 total designs across 3 rounds

    6. Click "Run Iterative Design"

    7. You'll see a series of toasts as each round runs
    8. Rounds typically take 1-3 minutes each on CPU

    9. Total: ~10 minutes

    10. Review the results panel:

    11. Initial score → Final score with improvement %
    12. Optimization history chart
    13. Best sequence per round

    14. The final structure is auto-added to your structures list

    15. Compare initial and final:

    16. Initial sequence is in your history
    17. Final sequence is the new active structure
    18. Use the vs button to compare them in 3D

    What you learned¶

    • The iterative optimization concept
    • How to read the score history
    • How to interpret improvement
    • The fold ↔ design feedback loop

    Try this variation¶

    Set rounds to 5, candidates to 10. Watch a longer optimization. See if it converges or keeps improving.

    Tutorial 5: Famous Mutations to Try¶

    These are real mutations from biology and disease. Try them in OpenDNA.

    Tumor suppressors and oncogenes¶

    KRAS G12D (most common cancer mutation): 1. Import → kras 2. Mutate → G12D 3. This is the famous G12D KRAS mutation found in pancreatic cancer

    p53 R175H (tumor suppressor mutation): 1. Import → p53 2. Mutate → R175H 3. This is one of the most common p53 mutations in human cancer

    Insulin variants¶

    Insulin lispro (Humalog) — switch the position of B28 and B29 to make a fast-acting insulin - Import insulin → mutate K28P then P29K (sequentially)

    Hemoglobin S (sickle cell)¶

    • Import hemoglobin_alpha → not the right chain
    • Try fetching P68871 (hemoglobin beta) → mutate E6V

    CFTR ΔF508 (cystic fibrosis)¶

    • Fetch P13569 (CFTR)
    • Note: very large protein, don't fold on CPU

    Tutorial 6: Use the Command Palette (3 minutes)¶

    Goal: Master the keyboard-driven workflow.

    Steps¶

    1. Open the palette anywhere: Press Ctrl+K (or Cmd+K on Mac)

    2. Try fuzzy search:

    3. Type "ubiq" → finds "Import ubiquitin"
    4. Type "fold" → finds the fold action

    5. Type "score" → finds the score action

    6. Quick imports:

    7. Ctrl+K → "import gfp" → Enter

    8. The sequence loads automatically

    9. Quick analysis:

    10. Ctrl+K → "analyze" → Enter

    11. Skips clicking

    12. Navigate without a mouse:

    13. Ctrl+K → up/down arrows → Enter
    14. Esc to close

    Power user tip¶

    With single-letter shortcuts (only when not focused on an input): - F = fold current sequence - S = score - A = analyze - Esc = close everything

    Tutorial 7: The Protein Academy (15 minutes)¶

    Goal: Learn protein basics through interactive games.

    Steps¶

    1. Click Academy in the header

    2. Level 1: Amino Acid Match (5 min, +50 XP):

    3. Click letters and names to match them up
    4. 8 random amino acids per game
    5. +10 score per match

    6. Complete to earn 50 XP

    7. Level 2: Memory Quiz (3 min, +75 XP):

    8. 5 multiple-choice questions about amino acid properties

    9. Test your knowledge of charge, structure, function

    10. Level 3: Sequence Reader (5 min, +100 XP):

    11. Tutorial on reading protein sequences
    12. Learn the meaning of each letter

    13. Pattern recognition tips

    14. Watch your XP grow in the header badge

    Educational notes¶

    • The 20 standard amino acids are the building blocks of all proteins
    • Their letter codes (A, C, D, E, F, G, H, I, K, L, M, N, P, Q, R, S, T, V, W, Y) are universally used
    • Each has different properties: charge, size, hydrophobicity, special function
    • Reading a sequence and predicting properties is the foundational skill

    Tutorial 8: Compare Two Structures (10 minutes)¶

    Goal: Use OpenDNA to compare a wild-type protein and a mutant.

    Steps¶

    1. Fold the wild-type:
    2. Paste: MKTVRQERLKSIVRILER
    3. Click Predict Structure

    4. Wait for the fold

    5. Apply a mutation:

    6. Mutate field: K2D
    7. Click Apply Mutation & Refold

    8. Wait for the new fold

    9. Compare them:

    10. Click the Structures tab
    11. You should see 2 structures
    12. Click vs on the first one (the wild-type)

    13. The viewer splits into two

    14. Visually inspect:

    15. Are they similar topology?
    16. Did the mutation cause any visible changes?

    17. Use both viewers' rotation independently

    18. Quantitative comparison (via API): 

      curl -X POST http://localhost:8765/v1/compare \
  -H "Content-Type: application/json" \
  -d '{"pdb_a": "...", "pdb_b": "..."}'
      Returns RMSD, secondary structure identity, radius of gyration.

    Tutorial 9: Estimate the Cost of Your Protein¶

    Goal: Find out how much it would cost to actually synthesize and produce your designed protein.

    Steps¶

    1. Have a sequence loaded (any size)

    2. Click Cost & Carbon Estimate

    3. Review:

    4. Synthesis costs from Twist Bioscience, IDT, GenScript
    5. Cheapest vendor highlighted

    6. Carbon footprint comparison: CPU vs GPU compute

    7. Use this to plan budget for wet-lab experiments

    What the estimates mean¶

    • Synthesis = ordering the DNA gene that codes for your protein
    • After ordering: clone into vector → transform into E. coli → grow → purify → assay
    • Total wet-lab cost typically 5-20x the synthesis cost

    Tutorial 10: Save and Reload a Project¶

    Goal: Don't lose your work between sessions.

    Steps¶

    1. Build up some structures (fold a few proteins)

    2. Save:

    3. Press Ctrl+Shift+S (or use Cmd+K → "save project")
    4. Enter a project name (e.g. my_first_project)

    5. Toast confirms save

    6. Find your saved project:

    7. Open the Dashboard (header button)

    8. "Saved Projects" section lists all your projects

    9. Load a project (manually via API for now): 

      curl -X POST http://localhost:8765/v1/projects/load \
  -d '{"name":"my_first_project"}'

    10. UI auto-load is coming in v0.3

    Where projects live¶

    ~/.opendna/projects/<name>/workspace.json

    Tutorial 11: Install and use DiffDock via the Component Manager¶

    Goal: Install the DiffDock docking engine from inside the desktop app and dock a small-molecule ligand against a folded protein.

    Prerequisites¶

    • Desktop app or browser UI running
    • ~1.5 GB free disk space
    • A folded structure (we'll use ubiquitin from the Import tab)

    Steps¶

    1. Open the Component Manager
    2. Click Components in the header

    3. You'll see 11 engine cards. Filter to the Docking category.

    4. Install DiffDock

    5. Click Install on the DiffDock card
    6. A progress bar streams from POST /v1/components/install
    7. Wait ~2 minutes for the weights to download (~1.1 GB)

    8. Status changes to Installed when done

    9. Load your receptor

    10. Close the Component Manager
    11. Import tab → click ubiquitin

    12. Click Predict Structure (ESMFold must be installed — if not, install it first)

    13. Supply a ligand

    14. In the sidebar, open the Docking section
    15. Paste a SMILES string for your ligand: 
      CC(=O)OC1=CC=CC=C1C(=O)O

    16. (That's aspirin — a nice small test ligand.)

    17. Run the dock via CLI equivalent 

      curl -X POST http://127.0.0.1:8765/v1/dock \
  -H "Content-Type: application/json" \
  -d '{
    "receptor_pdb": "...",
    "ligand_smiles": "CC(=O)OC1=CC=CC=C1C(=O)O",
    "engine": "diffdock",
    "num_poses": 10
  }'

    18. View the results

    19. The viewer displays the top-scoring pose by default
    20. Use the pose slider at the bottom to flip through ranked poses
    21. Each pose carries an affinity estimate

    What you learned¶

    • How the Component Manager gates heavy ML engines
    • How DiffDock differs from AutoDock Vina (diffusion vs. lattice search)
    • How to inspect pose rankings from the UI and the API

    Next¶

    • Try AutoDock Vina via "engine": "vina" and compare
    • Feed the best pose into an MD run to see if it stays bound

    Tutorial 12: Build a fold → design → evaluate workflow in the Visual Editor¶

    Goal: Use the visual workflow editor to build a reproducible pipeline without writing any Python.

    Steps¶

    1. Open the editor
    2. Click Workflow in the header

    3. The canvas opens empty

    4. Load a template

    5. Click Templates → Fold → Design → Evaluate

    6. A pre-built 5-node graph appears: 

      [Sequence] → [Fold] → [Design] → [Fold] → [Score]

    7. Configure the Sequence node

    8. Double-click the Sequence node

    9. Paste ubiquitin: 

      MQIFVKTLTGKTITLEVEPSDTIENVKAKIQDKEGIPPDQQRLIFAGKQLEDGRTLSDYNIQKESTLHLVLRLRGG

    10. Configure the Design node

    11. Double-click the Design node
    12. Set engine: esm_if1

    13. Candidates: 10

    14. Run

    15. Click Run in the toolbar
    16. Nodes light up green as they complete. Progress is streamed via WebSocket.

    17. Total runtime: ~4 minutes on GPU, ~25 minutes on CPU

    18. Save the workflow

    19. Click Save → YAML
    20. Name it ubiquitin-redesign.yaml

    21. It lands in ~/.opendna/workflows/

    22. Inspect the provenance

    23. Click History in the bottom bar
    24. You'll see one DAG node per workflow step with inputs, outputs, and hashes

    25. Drag the timeline handle back to the Design step to inspect the 10 candidates

    26. Re-run from YAML via CLI 

      opendna workflow run ~/.opendna/workflows/ubiquitin-redesign.yaml

    What you learned¶

    • How to use templates as a starting point
    • How node types and edges enforce type safety
    • How provenance is automatically recorded
    • How to save / replay workflows without opening the UI

    Tutorial 13: Real-time collaboration with a teammate via Yjs¶

    Goal: Co-edit a project with a colleague in real time.

    Steps¶

    1. Start a room
    2. Open your project in OpenDNA
    3. Click Collab in the header
    4. Click New room

    5. Copy the room URL (it looks like opendna://collab/8a4f...)

    6. Share it

    7. Send the URL to your teammate via Slack / Teams / email

    8. They click it, the app opens, and they join the same project

    9. Use the Notes tab

    10. Both of you can type in the shared Markdown document
    11. You'll see each other's cursors live

    12. Try **bold**, # headings, ```python code blocks

    13. Pin residue comments

    14. Click residue K48 in the viewer
    15. Click Comment
    16. Type "Suspected catalytic nucleophile — let's mutate to R"

    17. Your teammate sees the comment instantly and can reply

    18. Simulate offline tolerance

    19. Disable your Wi-Fi
    20. Keep editing the notes

    21. Re-enable Wi-Fi — the Yjs CRDT auto-merges without conflicts

    22. Verify sync via the API 

      curl http://127.0.0.1:8765/v1/collab/rooms/<room-id>
      Shows current participants and last-update timestamp.

    What you learned¶

    • How Yjs CRDTs give conflict-free real-time editing
    • How comments are pinned to residues and persist forever
    • How the system tolerates network interruptions

    Tutorial 14: Search UniProt for cancer proteins and fold the top hit¶

    Goal: Use the unified search to find a relevant protein and fold it.

    Steps¶

    1. Open the search overlay

    2. Click Search in the header (or press Ctrl+Shift+F)

    3. Search with filters

    4. Query: cancer kinase
    5. Check Reviewed only
    6. Organism: Homo sapiens
    7. Length: 100–500

    8. Click Search

    9. Inspect results

    10. You'll see ~20 Swiss-Prot reviewed hits
    11. Each row: accession, name, length, review badge

    12. Click the first result — say, BRAF_HUMAN

    13. Fold it

    14. Back in the Tools tab the sequence is loaded
    15. Click Predict Structure

    16. ESMFold runs (installed from Tutorial 11 or Component Manager)

    17. Analyze

    18. Click Full Analysis Suite

    19. Look at disorder (BRAF has a disordered N-terminus), binding pockets (the ATP pocket should rank #1)

    20. Equivalent cURL 

      curl "http://127.0.0.1:8765/v1/search/uniprot?q=cancer+kinase&reviewed=true&organism=Homo+sapiens"

    What you learned¶

    • How to compose filters in the unified search
    • How results integrate seamlessly with the Tools tab
    • How to reach the same functionality from the API

    Tutorial 15: Enable PQC auth and create API keys¶

    Goal: Turn on post-quantum authentication and mint a scoped API key.

    Prerequisites¶

    pip install "opendna[pqc]"
# macOS
brew install liboqs
# Debian/Ubuntu
sudo apt install liboqs-dev

    Steps¶

    1. Start the server with auth required 

      export OPENDNA_AUTH_REQUIRED=1
export OPENDNA_AUTH_DB=~/.opendna/auth.db
python -c "from opendna.api.server import start_server; start_server(port=8765)"

    2. Register the first user (admin) 

      curl -X POST http://127.0.0.1:8765/v1/auth/register \
  -H "Content-Type: application/json" \
  -d '{"username": "alice", "password": "correct horse battery staple"}'

    Response: 

    {
  "user_id": "u_001",
  "role": "admin",
  "api_key": "odna_pqc_9f3a...",
  "ml_dsa_public_key": "...base64..."
}

    Save the API key now — it is only shown once.

    1. Test it 

      export KEY="odna_pqc_9f3a..."
curl http://127.0.0.1:8765/v1/auth/me -H "Authorization: Bearer $KEY"

    2. Create a scoped API key for a CI pipeline 

      curl -X POST http://127.0.0.1:8765/v1/auth/api-keys \
  -H "Authorization: Bearer $KEY" \
  -H "Content-Type: application/json" \
  -d '{
    "name": "github-ci",
    "scopes": ["fold", "analyze"],
    "expires_in_days": 90
  }'

    3. Register a second user 

      curl -X POST http://127.0.0.1:8765/v1/auth/register \
  -H "Authorization: Bearer $KEY" \
  -H "Content-Type: application/json" \
  -d '{"username": "bob", "password": "..."}'
      Bob is created as a regular user (not admin) because he is registered by alice.

    4. Verify the audit log 

      opendna audit verify
opendna audit tail --n 20

    What you learned¶

    • How to enable PQC auth
    • How the first-user-is-admin pattern works
    • How to mint scoped API keys for automation
    • How the hash-chained audit log is verified end-to-end

    Tutorial 16: Mint a Zenodo DOI for your project¶

    Goal: Publish a reproducible package of your project to Zenodo and get a citable DOI.

    Prerequisites¶

    • A Zenodo account (free at zenodo.org)
    • A personal access token from zenodo.org/account/settings/applications/
    • An OpenDNA project with at least one folded structure

    Steps¶

    1. Set your token 

      export ZENODO_TOKEN="your_token_here"

    2. Open the Lab Notebook

    3. In the desktop app, click Save → Notebook

    4. Fill in at least:

      • Title (e.g. "Computational redesign of ubiquitin K48")
      • Authors (e.g. "Alice Smith; Bob Jones")
      • Description / abstract
      • Keywords (comma-separated)

    5. Click Publish → Zenodo

    6. OpenDNA bundles:
      • Provenance DAG (YAML)
      • Every structure (PDB)
      • Every figure (PNG + SVG)
      • Notebook (Markdown + PDF)
      • Workflow YAML (if present)
      • CycloneDX SBOM
      • Manifest with SHA-256 hashes

    7. Uploaded as a single .zip

    8. Confirm

    9. The response dialog shows the DOI: 
      10.5281/zenodo.12345678

    10. Click Copy DOI or Open on Zenodo

    11. CLI alternative 

      opendna publish zenodo \
  --project ~/.opendna/projects/ubiquitin_study \
  --title "Computational redesign of ubiquitin K48" \
  --author "Alice Smith" \
  --keyword "protein,design,ESMFold"

    12. Re-fetch your published package later 

      opendna fetch zenodo 10.5281/zenodo.12345678
      This pulls the .zip, verifies hashes, and reconstructs the project locally — a complete reproducibility round-trip.

    What you learned¶

    • How the lab notebook bundles a project into a single artifact
    • How to mint a DOI programmatically
    • How to round-trip a published project for reproducibility
